## 1. IRAQ WAR (2003–2011)

### Conflict Identification
- **Exact name**: Iraq War / Second Gulf War
- **Operation names**: Operation Iraqi Freedom (US/CENTCOM), Operation TELIC (UK), Operation FALCONER (Australia)
- **Start date**: March 20, 2003, ~20:00 Baghdad time
- **Full conflict period**: March 20, 2003 – December 18, 2011
- **Commanding authority**: US Central Command (CENTCOM); Gen. Tommy Franks

### Coalition Members
- **Combat forces in invasion**: United States (~148,000), United Kingdom (~45,000), Australia (~2,000), Poland (~194 special forces)
- **"Coalition of the Willing"**: 49 countries listed; only 4 had combat forces during invasion
- **Primary responsibility**: US and UK forces conducted overwhelming majority of strikes

### Children Killed — First 20 Days (Mar 20–Apr 9, 2003)
- **Min**: ~100 (derived from IBC ~6,616 invasion-phase civilian deaths, ~5% child proportion per Hicks et al. 2011, scaled to 20/42 days)
- **Max**: ~200 (same methodology, upper range accounting for disproportionate violence of "Shock and Awe" phase)
- **Full invasion phase (42 days)**: estimated ~250–450 children killed
- **NOTE**: These are derived estimates. No source publishes a disaggregated child count for this exact window.

### Children Injured — First 20 Days
- **Min**: ~300 / **Max**: ~800 (inferred from 3:1–4:1 wounded-to-killed ratio; no direct data exists)

### Key Facts
- "Shock and Awe": ~1,700 air sorties + 504 cruise missiles in first 2 days against a city of 5 million
- US used 10,782 cluster munitions containing ~1.8 million submunitions (HRW "Off Target" 2003)
- All 50 "decapitation strikes" against Iraqi leadership missed targets, killing civilians instead
- General Tommy Franks: "We don't do body counts"
- Kofi Annan (2004): "From the Charter point of view, it was illegal"

### IHL Notes
- US has NOT ratified Additional Protocol I, Convention on the Rights of the Child, or Rome Statute
- Cluster munitions in populated areas (Baghdad, Basra, Hilla)
- Attacks on civilian infrastructure (electrical grids, water treatment, telecommunications)

## 2. AFGHANISTAN (2001–2021)

### Conflict Identification
- **Exact name**: War in Afghanistan / Operation Enduring Freedom
- **Operation names**: Operation Enduring Freedom (OEF, Oct 2001–Dec 2014), Operation Freedom's Sentinel (OFS, Jan 2015–Aug 2021)
- **Start date**: October 7, 2001, ~20:45 local Afghan time
- **Full conflict period**: October 7, 2001 – August 30, 2021 (19 years, 10 months, 23 days)
- **Commanding authority**: US Central Command (CENTCOM)

### Coalition Members (First 20 Days)
- **United States** — overwhelming majority of strikes (B-2, B-1B, B-52 bombers; carrier aircraft; Tomahawk cruise missiles)
- **United Kingdom** — Tomahawk cruise missiles from submarines on opening night
- Australia, Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Turkey contributed soon after
- Northern Alliance (Afghan anti-Taliban ground forces)

### Children Killed — First 20 Days (Oct 7–27, 2001)
- **Min**: ~120 (demographic extrapolation from low civilian death estimate)
- **Max**: ~750 (demographic extrapolation from high civilian death estimate)
- **Methodology**: Afghanistan's population was ~48-50% under 18. Total civilians killed Oct 7–27 estimated 300–1,500. Children estimated at 40-50% of residential bombing casualties.
- **NOTE**: These are methodologically derived estimates, NOT verified counts. UNAMA didn't begin systematic civilian casualty documentation until 2007.

### Children Injured — First 20 Days
- **Min**: ~240 / **Max**: ~2,250 (applying 2:1–3:1 injury ratios; near-total collapse of Afghan medical system)

### Key Facts
- Marc Herold (U of New Hampshire) estimated ~3,000–3,600 civilians killed Oct 7–Dec 7, 2001 — no age breakdown
- Documented child-casualty incidents: Karam village (Oct 11), Kabul neighborhoods (Oct 13), Chowkar-Karez (Oct 21-22)
- US dropped 1,228 cluster munitions (248,056 bomblets) — HRW "Fatally Flawed" (2002)
- No UNAMA monitoring until 2007; no independent observers in strike zones

## 3. LIBYA (2011)

### Conflict Identification
- **Exact name**: 2011 Military Intervention in Libya (First Libyan Civil War)
- **Operation names**: Operation Odyssey Dawn (US/AFRICOM, Mar 19–31), Operation Unified Protector (NATO, Mar 31–Oct 31), Operation Ellamy (UK), Operation Harmattan (France), Operation Mobile (Canada)
- **Start date**: March 19, 2011, ~16:45 UTC (French Rafale jets first to strike)
- **Full conflict period**: March 19, 2011 – October 31, 2011
- **Authorization**: UNSC Resolution 1973 (March 17, 2011)

### Coalition Members
- United States, France, United Kingdom, Canada, Italy, Denmark, Norway, Belgium, Spain, Netherlands, Turkey, Greece (NATO members)
- Qatar, UAE, Jordan, Sweden (non-NATO participants)
- Total campaign: ~26,500 sorties including ~9,700 strike sorties

### Children Killed — First 20 Days (Mar 19–Apr 8, 2011)
- **NO DATA EXISTS.** No organization produced disaggregated child casualty figures for this period.
- Airwars was founded in 2014; no real-time monitoring existed
- HRW documented 72 civilians killed at 8 sites across full campaign; major child incidents (e.g., Majer, Aug 2011) were later
- First-phase strikes targeted air defenses and military columns, not urban areas

### Why the Data Gap
- NATO actively refused to count or acknowledge civilian casualties throughout
- Limited media/investigator access to western Libya (Gaddafi-controlled)
- Pre-Airwars era — open-source investigation tools not yet mature
- Libyan state collapse destroyed record-keeping

### IHL Notes
- NATO Secretary General Rasmussen repeatedly claimed zero civilian casualties — contradicted by every independent investigation
- HRW "Unacknowledged Deaths" (2012) documented NATO's refusal of post-strike civilian harm assessments

## 5. SOMALIA DRONE STRIKES (2001–PRESENT)

### Conflict Identification
- **Exact name**: US Military Operations in Somalia / War on Terror — Somalia theater
- **First confirmed US strike**: January 7, 2007 (AC-130 gunship, Ras Kamboni, southern Somalia)
- **Operation names**: Pre-AFRICOM strikes under CENTCOM/Combined Joint Task Force–Horn of Africa (CJTF-HOA), Camp Lemonnier, Djibouti. AFRICOM established October 1, 2007.
- **Legal authority**: 2001 AUMF

### Coalition/Supporting Countries
- United States (strikes), Ethiopia (ground invasion Dec 2006), Kenya (border ops), Djibouti (basing), UK/France (intelligence)
- AMISOM (later ATMIS): Uganda, Burundi, Kenya troop contributors

### Children Killed — First 20 Days (Jan 7–27, 2007)
- **NO DISAGGREGATED DATA EXISTS.** The data environment is characterized as a "counter-forensic environment."
- Remote location (Ras Kamboni), active combat zone, no independent monitoring, covert operations, Somali state collapse
- Total civilian casualties from Jan 7 strikes: "single digits to several dozen" — no age breakdown

### Key Facts
- First strikes used AC-130 gunships, NOT drones. Drone-specific strikes began ~2011.
- AFRICOM historically claimed zero/near-zero civilian casualties — contradicted by Airwars, Amnesty, TBIJ
- 2017–2020: More US strikes in Somalia than all prior years combined (Trump "area of active hostilities" designation)

## CROSS-CUTTING FINDINGS

### 1. The Hierarchy of Counting
The data quality correlates inversely with how much the US controls the information environment:
- **Best data**: Israel-Gaza (multiple independent monitors, dense population, cell networks), Ukraine (OHCHR HRMMU present since 2014)
- **Worst data**: Somalia (counter-forensic environment), Libya (pre-Airwars era), Afghanistan 2001 (no observers), Colombia (gradual escalation, no monitoring)

### 2. The "We Don't Do Body Counts" Doctrine
The US military's systematic refusal to count civilian casualties (Gen. Franks, CENTCOM zero-casualty claims in Syria, AFRICOM denials in Somalia) is a recurring structural feature across all conflicts where the US is the direct perpetrator.

### 3. US Treaty Non-Ratification
The United States has NOT ratified:
- Convention on the Rights of the Child (sole non-party among UN members)
- Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions
- Rome Statute of the ICC
- Convention on Cluster Munitions
These are the four primary international instruments for protecting children in armed conflict.

### 4. Israel-Gaza as Outlier
The Oct 7–27, 2023 period in Gaza produced **more documented child casualties in 20 days (~2,800–3,500) than all other conflicts in this dataset combined.** This is corroborated by UNICEF's assessment that it was "unprecedented in modern conflict."

### 5. Cluster Munitions: A Cross-Conflict Child Killer
US-supplied or US-used cluster munitions appear in Iraq (1.8M submunitions), Afghanistan (248K bomblets), Lebanon (~4M submunitions, ~1M unexploded), and Yemen (CBU-105 documented). These weapons have a documented pattern of disproportionate harm to children, who are attracted to small, brightly colored unexploded submunitions.
